\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
\thispagestyle{fancy}

As part of my graduation project at \textbf{\myCompany}, I designed and developed
a full-stack platform named \textbf{Fakerni}, dedicated to the collaborative and
real-time management of shared lists --- called \emph{Fakras} --- for any kind of
group: a family, a community, an association, or an organization. The project
centralizes group membership with role-based access control, enables members to
create and share shopping and to-do lists, track budgets and spending, and
coordinate tasks in real time across the web and mobile.

\medskip
Fakerni distinguishes itself through several advanced capabilities: an AI
assistant (powered by a large language model with structured output) that turns
free text and receipt photos into structured items; a real-time collaboration
layer built on WebSockets with live presence; a centralized authorization module;
budget tracking with fair expense-splitting; and role-based analytics dashboards.
A dynamic multilingual interface supporting English, French, and Arabic (with
right-to-left layout) was also integrated to make the platform accessible to a
wide range of users.

\medskip
The backend was developed with \textbf{Django} and \textbf{Django REST Framework}
(with \textbf{Django Channels} for the real-time layer), the web client with
\textbf{React}, and the mobile client with \textbf{React Native (Expo)}, over a
\textbf{PostgreSQL} database, following the principles of the \textbf{Agile Scrum}
methodology. This project enabled me to strengthen and apply my knowledge in
full-stack development, real-time systems, Role-Based Access Control (RBAC),
artificial-intelligence integration, database design, software architecture, and
agile project management.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Fakerni, Shared Lists, Collaborative Platform,
Real-Time, WebSockets, Role-Based Access Control, Artificial Intelligence,
Django, React, React Native, PostgreSQL.

\chapter*{General Introduction}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{General Introduction}

In an increasingly connected world, the way in which groups of people coordinate
their everyday responsibilities has become a genuine challenge. Deciding what to
buy, who is responsible for which task, and how shared expenses are divided still
largely relies on scattered and informal tools: paper notes, instant-messaging
conversations, and spreadsheets. These tools fragment information across several
places, offer no real-time synchronization, provide no notion of roles or
permissions, and give no shared view of budgets or of who did what. The result is
duplicated effort, forgotten items, and recurring misunderstandings.

It is within this context that the \textbf{Fakerni} project was initiated and
developed at \textbf{\myCompany}, a company specialized in the design and delivery
of modern digital solutions. The word ``Fakerni'' means \emph{``remind me''}, and
a ``Fakra'' is a single shared list --- literally an \emph{idea} or a
\emph{reminder} that the members of a group keep together. The primary objective
of the Fakerni application is to design and implement a centralized,
cross-platform system that enables:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item the real-time creation and sharing of collaborative lists among the
        members of any group;
  \item the structured management of groups, their members, and their roles
        (owner, admin, member) with fine-grained permissions;
  \item budget tracking, spending analytics, and fair expense-splitting;
  \item AI-assisted item entry from natural language and from receipt photographs;
  \item role-based dashboards that give each user a tailored overview of activity.
\end{itemize}

The application consolidates all of a group's coordination data into a single
source of truth that is served identically to a web client and to a mobile
client. Fakerni distinguishes itself through its real-time collaboration layer,
its centralized and consistent authorization model, and its genuinely useful AI
features, which reduce the friction of entering and organizing items.

In accordance with modern technological standards, the application was developed
using \textbf{Django / Django REST Framework} and \textbf{Django Channels} for a
secure, high-performance and real-time backend API; \textbf{React} for a
responsive single-page web interface; \textbf{React Native (Expo)} for the
cross-platform mobile application; and \textbf{PostgreSQL} as the relational
database management system ensuring data persistence and integrity. The real-time
layer is backed by a \textbf{Redis} channel layer, and the AI features rely on a
large language model with schema-constrained (structured) output.

The project was carried out following the \textbf{Agile Scrum} methodology,
allowing iterative and flexible development through multiple sprints, intermediate
deliverables, and continuous adaptation to requirements. This experience allowed
me to apply my web-development and information-systems skills across six coherent
sprints, from the secure foundation of authentication and access control to the
full real-time, AI-assisted collaboration cycle. This report presents the various
stages of the project, from the initial requirements analysis and technical design
through development, testing, and the integration of user-friendly and
high-performance interfaces.

\section{Project Overview}
\subsection{Current Situation}
Currently, most groups --- whether families sharing a household, roommates,
students working together, or the members of an association --- coordinate their
shared tasks and purchases using dispersed and informal tools such as
instant-messaging groups, paper lists, and spreadsheets. Reminders are sent as
chat messages that quickly scroll out of view; shopping lists are written on paper
and forgotten at home; and shared expenses are tracked, when they are tracked at
all, in ad-hoc spreadsheets. This makes coordination complex, limits visibility
over who is responsible for what, and provides no shared, real-time view of
spending.

\subsection{Analysis of the Current System}
This ad-hoc approach presents the following limitations:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Lack of a single, centralized list accessible to all members at once;
  \item No real-time synchronization --- changes made by one member are not seen
        instantly by the others;
  \item No notion of roles or permissions within the group, so anyone can alter
        or delete anything;
  \item No shared tracking of budgets, spending, or of who paid for what;
  \item Manual, repetitive, and error-prone entry of items, with no assistance;
  \item No consolidated overview or analytics of the group's activity over time.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Problem Statement}
Given the challenges identified above --- fragmented information, the absence of
real-time synchronization, the lack of roles and permissions, and the absence of
any shared financial view --- there is a clear need for a single, secure, and
cross-platform application that streamlines coordination between the members of a
group. The core problem statement of this project can therefore be formulated as
follows:

\begin{quote}\itshape
How can a centralized, real-time, and AI-assisted platform be developed to enable
any group to create and share collaborative lists, coordinate tasks with
role-based permissions, track budgets and spending, and stay perfectly
synchronized across web and mobile devices?
\end{quote}

\subsection{Proposed Solution}
To address these challenges, we designed and developed a cross-platform
application named \textbf{Fakerni} with the following features:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Group management with invitation codes and Role-Based Access Control
        (owner / admin / member);
  \item Shared lists (Fakras) with items, categories, attachments, due dates, and
        recurrence;
  \item Real-time collaboration and live presence over authenticated WebSockets;
  \item Budget tracking, spending analytics, over-budget alerts, and fair
        expense-splitting;
  \item AI-assisted item entry (from text and from receipt photos), item
        suggestions, smart commands, and a spending digest;
  \item Role-based interactive dashboards and push notifications;
  \item A multilingual interface (English, French, and Arabic) with full
        right-to-left support.
\end{itemize}

\section{Project Management Methodology}
\subsection{Different Project-Management Approaches}
The success of a software development project largely depends on the
project-management methodology adopted. Several approaches can be used to organize
and control the development process; the most common are the Waterfall approach,
the Agile Scrum methodology, and the Spiral model.

\paragraph{Waterfall Approach.}
The Waterfall model follows a strictly sequential process composed of several
phases --- requirements analysis, system design, development, testing,
deployment, and maintenance --- where each phase must be completed before moving
to the next. Its advantages are a clear structure, comprehensive documentation,
and easy progress monitoring; its limitations are its limited flexibility when
requirements change, the late discovery of errors during the testing phase, and
its unsuitability for dynamic and evolving projects.

\paragraph{Agile Scrum Methodology.}
Scrum is an iterative and incremental framework derived from the Agile principles.
The project is divided into short development cycles called \emph{sprints}, during
each of which a functional increment of the product is delivered. It is organized
around a Product Backlog and regular events (Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint
Review, Sprint Retrospective). Its advantages are high flexibility, rapid
adaptation to change, continuous improvement, and early delivery of value; its
limitations are the need for continuous involvement and strong organization.

\paragraph{Spiral Model.}
The Spiral model combines characteristics of both the Waterfall and iterative
approaches; development progresses through successive cycles, each including
planning, risk analysis, engineering, and evaluation. It is particularly suitable
for large-scale or high-risk projects because it emphasizes risk assessment, but
it is more complex and costly to manage and requires expertise in risk
identification.

\subsection{Chosen Methodology}
For the development of Fakerni, the \textbf{Agile Scrum} methodology was adopted.
This choice was motivated by the need to build the application incrementally while
continuously adapting to feedback and to evolving requirements. The project was
organized into six sprints, each focusing on a coherent set of functionalities:
authentication and access control; groups and shared lists; budget and analytics;
AI-assisted features; real-time collaboration; and dashboards with the
multilingual interface. At the end of each sprint, a functional and validated
increment of the application was delivered and evaluated. This methodology
facilitated continuous communication, rapid problem resolution, progressive
feature delivery, and a strong alignment between the developed solution and the
target users' needs.

\section{Requirements Specification}
\subsection{Functional Requirements}
The functional requirements define the services and behaviours expected from
Fakerni. Based on the needs of the target users, the following core
functionalities were identified and implemented:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Authentication and Access Control:} secure registration and login
        using an email address and password, JWT-based sessions with silent token
        refresh, password reset via a one-time code (OTP), and a centralized
        Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) enforced on every request.
  \item \textbf{Group Management:} create a group, join an existing group via a
        time-limited invitation code, and manage members and their roles (owner,
        admin, member).
  \item \textbf{Shared List (Fakra) Management:} create, edit, share, archive,
        duplicate, and delete lists, including due dates and recurrence.
  \item \textbf{Item Management:} add, edit, complete/undo, delete, and assign
        items; attach photographs; scan barcodes; and search and filter items.
  \item \textbf{Budget and Analytics:} set a budget per list, track spending
        against it, receive over-budget alerts, view monthly and per-category
        analytics, and split shared expenses fairly among the members.
  \item \textbf{AI-Assisted Features:} create structured items from
        natural-language text, extract items from a receipt photo, obtain item
        suggestions, issue smart natural-language commands, and generate a
        spending digest.
  \item \textbf{Real-Time Collaboration:} instant synchronization of all changes
        and live presence indicators across every connected client.
  \item \textbf{Dashboards and Notifications:} role-based overview dashboards with
        key indicators, and push notifications for relevant events.
  \item \textbf{Multilingual Interface:} dynamically switch the display language
        between English, French, and Arabic (right-to-left).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Non-Functional Requirements}
The non-functional requirements describe the quality attributes and technical
constraints that the platform must satisfy:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Security:} all API endpoints are protected by JWT authentication;
        a centralized authorization module enforces RBAC; passwords are hashed;
        sensitive values (reset codes, device tokens) are encrypted at rest; and
        every WebSocket connection is authenticated before it is accepted.
  \item \textbf{Performance:} the API uses query optimization (prefetching of
        related objects) and an asynchronous ASGI server to handle both REST and
        real-time traffic efficiently.
  \item \textbf{Scalability:} a stateless REST API and a Redis-backed channel
        layer allow the system to scale horizontally as the number of users grows.
  \item \textbf{Usability:} a modern, responsive interface with dark mode and full
        internationalization, usable comfortably on both web and mobile devices.
  \item \textbf{Reliability:} automatic WebSocket reconnection, transparent token
        refresh, and an automated test suite executed by continuous integration on
        every change.
  \item \textbf{Maintainability:} a modular architecture (separate Django apps and
        a single centralized permissions module) that eases evolution and bug
        fixing.
  \item \textbf{Portability:} the web and mobile clients share a single backend
        API, so business logic is written and maintained only once.
\end{itemize}

\section{Software Architecture}
Fakerni is built on a layered client--server architecture that separates the
presentation, business-logic, and data layers, and adds a dedicated real-time
channel. This approach ensures modularity, maintainability, and scalability.
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Presentation Layer:} a \textbf{React} web client and a
        \textbf{React Native (Expo)} mobile client. Both consume the same REST
        API and connect to the same WebSocket endpoints, so each platform is a
        thin, interchangeable face over one backend.
  \item \textbf{Business-Logic Layer:} a \textbf{Django / Django REST Framework}
        backend that processes requests, applies business rules, and enforces JWT
        authentication and centralized RBAC. \textbf{Django Channels} (over an
        ASGI server) handles the real-time layer.
  \item \textbf{Data Layer:} a \textbf{PostgreSQL} relational database for
        persistent data, and \textbf{Redis} as the channel layer that distributes
        real-time messages between connected clients.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Software Environment}
This section presents the technologies, frameworks, and tools used to develop
Fakerni.

\paragraph{Django \& Django REST Framework.}
Django is a high-level Python web framework, and Django REST Framework (DRF) is a
powerful toolkit built on top of it for creating REST APIs. In Fakerni, they form
the backbone of the backend: models describe the data, serializers translate
objects to and from JSON and validate input, and views handle every request. DRF
also provides the authentication and permission infrastructure that Fakerni builds
on.
\figph{logo-django.png}{Django / Django REST Framework}

\paragraph{Django Channels \& Daphne.}
Django Channels extends Django to support asynchronous protocols such as
WebSockets, and Daphne is the ASGI server that runs them. Together they power the
real-time layer of Fakerni, allowing the server to push updates to clients the
moment data changes.
\figph{logo-channels.png}{Django Channels}

\paragraph{PostgreSQL.}
PostgreSQL is a robust open-source relational database management system. In
Fakerni it stores all persistent data --- users, groups, memberships, lists,
items, attachments, and activity logs --- while guaranteeing data integrity through
constraints and relationships.
\figph{logo-postgres.png}{PostgreSQL}

\paragraph{Redis.}
Redis is an in-memory data store used in Fakerni as the \emph{channel layer}: it
distributes real-time messages between the different connections and server
processes, which is what allows an update from one user to reach every other
connected user.
\figph{logo-redis.png}{Redis}

\paragraph{React \& Vite.}
React is an open-source JavaScript library for building dynamic user interfaces,
and Vite is the fast build tool used to develop and bundle the web client. Fakerni
uses React to build the entire web front-end as a smooth single-page application,
with reusable components and efficient state management.
\figph{logo-react.png}{React}

\paragraph{React Native \& Expo.}
React Native allows the mobile application to be built with the same component
model as React, and Expo provides the tooling and native modules (camera,
notifications, secure storage). Fakerni's mobile client reuses the web's
architecture and API layer while offering a native experience.
\figph{logo-expo.png}{React Native \& Expo}

\paragraph{Google Gemini.}
Fakerni's AI features are powered by a large language model accessed through the
Gemini API. Every call uses \emph{structured output}: the model is given a strict
schema and must return valid, typed data, which makes the AI reliable enough to
drive real features such as item extraction and receipt scanning.
\figph{logo-gemini.png}{Google Gemini}

\paragraph{JSON Web Tokens (JWT), Docker, and GitHub Actions.}
JSON Web Tokens provide stateless authentication (a short-lived access token and a
longer-lived refresh token). Docker containerizes the application for consistent
deployment, and GitHub Actions runs the automated test suite as continuous
integration on every change. Development was carried out in \textbf{Visual Studio
Code} with \textbf{Git / GitHub} for version control.

\subsection{Refinement of the Use Case ``Authenticate''}
The use case \emph{Authenticate} illustrates the interaction between any user and
the Fakerni platform to gain secure access to the application. Authentication is
performed with an email address and a password; on success, the backend issues a
short-lived \emph{access} token and a longer-lived \emph{refresh} token, and the
client transparently renews the access token when it expires.

\subsection{Refinement of the Use Case ``Role-Based Access Control''}
Every ``who is allowed to do X'' decision is centralized in a single authorization
module. A user relates to a list through three relationships: being its
\emph{creator}, holding a \emph{group role} (owner, admin, or member), or having
been granted \emph{explicit shared access}. The backend enforces these rules on
every request; the client only uses them to hide the actions a user cannot
perform, so the enforcement is never the client's responsibility.

\section{Introduction}
Sprint 5 makes Fakerni genuinely collaborative in real time. Using WebSockets over

channel layer, and a live presence registry.

\section{Sprint 5 Analysis}
\subsection{Refinement of the Use Case ``Collaborate in Real Time''}
A custom middleware authenticates each WebSocket using the JWT passed in the
connection URL, and the consumer rejects any connection whose user cannot access
the requested list. Three channel scopes are used --- one per group, one per list,
and one per user --- so that each event reaches exactly the right audience. When a
REST action changes data, the server broadcasts an event to the relevant channel,
and every connected client updates instantly. A presence registry tracks who is
viewing each list and broadcasts the de-duplicated viewer list on every join and
leave.
\figph{uc-realtime.png}{Refinement Use Case Diagram: ``Collaborate in Real Time''}

\begin{table}[H]\centering
\caption{Use Case Description: ``Collaborate in Real Time''}

\hline
\textbf{Actors} & Owner, Admin, Member, Individual User \\ \hline
\textbf{Preconditions} & The user is authenticated and opens a list they can access. \\ \hline
\textbf{Postconditions} & The client is subscribed to live updates; presence is broadcast; every change is reflected instantly on all screens. \\ \hline
\textbf{Main Scenario} &
1. The client opens a WebSocket to the list, passing its JWT in the connection URL.
2. The middleware authenticates the token; the consumer verifies that the user can access the list.
3. The user joins the list's channel and is added to the presence registry, and the new viewer list is broadcast.
4. Any change (add, complete, edit, delete) performed by any member is broadcast to all subscribers, whose screens update immediately.
5. On leaving, the user is removed from the registry and the updated viewer list is broadcast. \\ \hline

\chapter*{General Conclusion and Perspectives}

\section*{General Conclusion}
This report was carried out as part of an internship project conducted at
\textbf{\myCompany}. Its objective was the design and development of
\textbf{Fakerni}, a collaborative, real-time, and AI-assisted platform enabling any
group to create and share lists, coordinate tasks with role-based permissions,
track budgets and spending, and stay perfectly synchronized across web and mobile.

To achieve this objective, we developed a layered client--server architecture in
which a single backend serves both a React web client and a React Native mobile
client. Particular effort was devoted to securing the application through a
JWT-based authentication system and a centralized Role-Based Access Control module;
to making collaboration genuinely live through authenticated WebSockets and a
presence registry; and to integrating reliable AI assistance through
schema-constrained model output. Budget tracking, spending analytics, and fair
expense-splitting were built on top of the data the application already collects,
and a dynamic multilingual interface (English, French, and Arabic with
right-to-left support) makes the platform broadly accessible.

Throughout the project, the \textbf{Agile Scrum} methodology ensured adaptability
to evolving requirements and the incremental delivery of validated features at the
end of each sprint. The use of modern technologies --- Django, Django Channels,
React, React Native, PostgreSQL, and Redis --- combined with a modular architecture
and a centralized permissions module, made it possible to build a robust, scalable,
and maintainable solution. The implementation required considerable research to
overcome challenges such as authenticated real-time communication, consistent
multi-role authorization across REST and WebSocket code paths, and the reliable
integration of a large language model. This experience strengthened my skills in
full-stack development, real-time systems, access control, AI integration, database
design, and agile project management.

\section*{Perspectives}
Several improvements and extensions can be envisioned for the Fakerni platform:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Offline Mode:} allow members to view and edit lists offline, with
        automatic synchronization when connectivity returns.
  \item \textbf{Multi-Server Presence:} move the presence registry to Redis so that
        live presence scales seamlessly across multiple backend processes.
  \item \textbf{Advanced Analytics:} add time-based trends, spending forecasts, and
        cross-group comparisons.
  \item \textbf{Third-Party Integrations:} connect to calendars, payment
        applications, and online-grocery services.
  \item \textbf{Automated Reports:} schedule the export of activity and spending
        summaries in PDF or spreadsheet formats, sent automatically by email.
\end{itemize}
